Key findings
The operator premium
Seasoned operators appear in 71% of all companies but jump to 89% of $100M+ companies. Seasoned executives follow the same pattern: 61% → 81%. Operator experience is the strongest predictor in this dataset.
+18pp liftFounder signal
Top company alumni: the biggest gap
Alumni from top companies appear in only 39% of all companies, but 71% of the $100M+ cohort — the single largest proportional jump of any signal. Google, Stripe, Palantir, and Uber are the highest-signal feeders.
+32pp liftBiggest gap
Top AI experience: the new differentiator
Only 12% of all companies have top AI experience on the team — but 40% of $100M+ funded companies do. This 3.3x concentration is the fastest-rising signal and likely still undervalued as an early signal.
3.3x concentrationRising signal
Sector overview
AI/ML leads with 516 companies, followed by FinTech/Crypto at 235, Consumer/Other at 161, HealthTech at 157, Cybersecurity at 142.
The Palantir mafia snapshot
Palantir alumni found in 78 companies — 3x overrepresented in $100M+ funded tier (5.6% → 16.9%)
Notable portfolio: ElevenLabs, LangChain, Decagon, Sierra, Together AI, Mistral AI, Tennr, Supabase, Cyera, Dust. These founders combine deep enterprise deployment instincts with AI/ML exposure — creating an outsized pattern of building in AI infrastructure and vertical AI applications.

The winner DNA — 12 signals
Operator-executive stack
89%
Have a seasoned operator on the team — up from 71% overall. Combined with a seasoned executive (81%), the operator-executive co-pattern defines winning teams more than any single background signal.
Top company alumni density
5.4
Average top company alumni mentions per winner vs 1.6 overall. Winners don't just have one ex-Google — they have five or more across the team. Alumni density, not just pedigree, is the real signal.
The Palantir coefficient
3×
Palantir alumni appear in 16.9% of $100M+ companies vs 5.6% overall — strongest company-specific signal in the dataset. Deployment-first, enterprise-fluent founders. Portfolio: ElevenLabs, Mistral, LangChain, Decagon, Sierra, Supabase.
Top AI experience lift
3.3×
Top AI experience in 40% of winners vs 12% overall. The fastest-rising signal. Having someone who shipped real AI product at OpenAI, DeepMind, or Google Brain is now table-stakes at the top tier.
Elite university density
86%
Of $100M+ companies have Stanford, Harvard, or MIT on the team — vs 58% overall. It's not where the founder went to school. It's building a team where elite alumni are the norm across the whole org.
Prior exit + VC-backed founder
41%
Of winners have both a prior exit AND prior VC-backed founder on the team — vs 34% overall. The combination, not just one signal, is what matters. Founders who've raised and exited understand timing, terms, and scale.
Cap table density
20
Average investors on a winner's cap table, vs 9 overall. More investors means more distribution, customer intros, and hiring pipelines. Winners build cap tables deliberately — not just to raise money.
Multi-fund validation
29%
Of winners have 3+ top-tier funds (a16z, Sequoia, Lightspeed, GC, Khosla, GV, Founders Fund, Coatue, Tiger, Thrive, DST, Accel) vs just 5.8% of all companies. When multiple franchises agree, it's not coincidence.
Headcount velocity
104%
Average 1-year headcount growth for winners, vs 70% overall and 41% for smaller companies. Winners grow their teams 2.5x faster than the bottom tier. 90-day headcount growth also nearly doubles: 19.8% vs 10.0%.
LinkedIn brand velocity
91%
Average 90-day LinkedIn follower growth vs 35% overall. Median follower count is 15,512 vs 3,366 for all companies — a 4.6x audience gap that compounds into hiring, press, and enterprise trust before a Series B.
Geographic concentration
36%
Of winners are in San Francisco (23%) or New York (13%). Despite remote-first narratives, the pipeline still concentrates in a handful of zip codes. London, Palo Alto, and Cambridge are the next tier.
2020–2021 founding window
58%
Of winners were founded in 2020 or 2021 — the COVID-era cohort with 4–5 years to compound. The 2022–2023 cohort is building. The best 2024–2025 companies haven't crossed $100M yet — but they will.

The winning company profile — in plain language
What a winner looks like on day one
A winning company was most likely founded in 2020 or 2021, in San Francisco or New York, in AI/ML, FinTech, or HealthTech. The founding team has at least one person with direct experience at Google, Palantir, Stripe, or SpaceX — not a mention on a resume but a meaningful tenure. At least one founder has raised before and ideally exited. The team has a Stanford, Harvard, or MIT footprint distributed across the org, not just the founder.

On the cap table: the company has attracted 20+ investors by the time it crosses $100M, including at least two major funds — and often a notable individual like Nat Friedman, Eric Schmidt, or Gokul Rajaram whose backing adds credibility beyond capital.

The company is growing its team at 104% year-over-year and its LinkedIn following at 91% over 90 days. Web traffic is 4.6x the dataset median. These aren't lagging indicators — they show up early and compound fast.

The one signal with zero predictive lift: deep technical background alone — present at 44% in both all companies and winners. Depth is table stakes. What separates winners is the operator layer built around it.
